The Solana MEV Landscape in 2026

What changed, who's winning, and where the opportunities are heading.

April 8, 2026 9 min read Industry Analysis

Two years ago, Solana MEV was dominated by a handful of sandwich bots running on Jito. Today, the ecosystem has fragmented into a complex infrastructure war where block engines compete for validator stake, flash loans have made arbitrage capital-efficient, and the barrier to entry has simultaneously lowered (better tooling) and raised (fiercer competition).

This is the state of Solana MEV as of April 2026 — what changed, what matters, and where the alpha is moving.

The Block Engine Wars

Jito's block engine dominated 2024 as the de facto standard for MEV transaction ordering on Solana. Searchers submitted bundles with tips, validators ran the Jito client, and the tip market determined inclusion priority. It was simple and effective.

Then came competition. Nozomi launched its own block engine with an alternative validator client, offering different fee structures and targeting validators not yet in the Jito network. Astralane entered with dedicated MEV infrastructure and direct validator partnerships. ZeroSlot focused on ultra-low-latency paths to specific slot leaders.

The result: searchers now need to submit through multiple block engines to maintain landing rates. A bot targeting only Jito misses 20-30% of slots where other engines have exclusive validator relationships. This is why our infrastructure runs 7 concurrent submitters — not out of excess, but necessity.

Tip Market Dynamics

With multiple competing engines, the tip market has become more efficient but also more complex. Average tips have increased as competition pushes searchers to bid more aggressively. However, smart tip strategies now factor in:

  • Which engine has the current slot leader
  • Historical landing rates per engine per validator
  • Real-time congestion signals
  • Expected profit margins with dynamic tip ceilings

The Rise of Flash Loan Arbitrage

Perhaps the biggest shift in Solana MEV economics has been the adoption of flash loans for arbitrage. Led primarily by Marginfi's flash loan primitive, searchers can now borrow millions in SOL or USDC within a single transaction, execute an arbitrage, repay the loan, and pocket the profit — all without deploying any capital.

This has had profound effects:

  • Lower barrier to entry: You don't need $100K+ in working capital to run an arb bot
  • Larger position sizes: Flash loans let you take maximum size on every opportunity
  • Increased competition: More participants chasing the same opportunities
  • Compressed margins: Profit per opportunity has decreased as more capital competes

The net effect is that infrastructure quality matters more than capital. The bot with better latency, more submitters, and smarter routing wins — regardless of how much capital it holds.

DEX Landscape Evolution

The DEX ecosystem on Solana has expanded significantly, creating more arbitrage surfaces:

PumpSwap

The migration of Pump.fun tokens from bonding curves to AMM pools (PumpSwap) created a massive new arbitrage surface. These pools handle high-volume memecoin trading with significant price inefficiency. PumpSwap has become one of the most active DEXes for MEV activity.

Meteora DLMM

Meteora's Dynamic Liquidity Market Maker introduced bin-based pricing that creates unique arbitrage patterns. The discrete price bins mean that price updates happen in steps rather than continuously, creating brief windows where Meteora prices diverge from continuous AMMs.

Concentrated Liquidity Everywhere

Both Raydium (CLMM) and Orca (Whirlpools) have pushed concentrated liquidity as their primary product. This makes arbitrage more complex — you need to traverse tick arrays and calculate quotes through multiple price ranges — but also more profitable because liquidity concentration amplifies price impact and creates larger cross-DEX discrepancies.

Infrastructure Advances

Geyser gRPC Maturation

The Yellowstone Geyser plugin has matured from experimental to production-critical infrastructure. Multiple providers (Helius, Triton, Syndica) offer hosted Geyser endpoints with sub-second account update delivery. Self-hosted validators with Geyser plugins remain the fastest option but require significant operational overhead.

Shred-Level Data

Advanced searchers now decode shreds (the raw data units validators produce) before they're assembled into blocks. This provides the earliest possible signal of new transactions hitting the network — often 50-100ms before Geyser delivers the processed account update.

Local Simulation

Rather than relying on RPC simulateTransaction, top searchers run local Solana validators in simulation mode. This eliminates network round-trip for simulation and allows thousands of simulations per second — critical for evaluating complex multi-hop paths.

Strategy Shifts

From Sandwiches to Backrunning

Sandwich attacks — front-running a user's swap to extract value — have declined as a percentage of total MEV. DEX aggregators and wallets increasingly route through Jito with MEV protection, making sandwich attacks harder to execute. The focus has shifted to backrunning: detecting large swaps and arbitraging the resulting price impact without harming the original user.

Long-Tail Tokens

Major pairs (SOL/USDC, SOL/USDT) are extremely competitive. The alpha has moved to long-tail tokens — memecoins, new launches, low-liquidity pairs where fewer bots compete. Success here requires monitoring more pools and executing on more diverse path combinations.

Cross-Protocol Strategies

The most profitable strategies now span multiple protocol types: arbitrage between DEXes and lending platforms, liquidation + position takeover combos, and cross-margin opportunities. These require deeper protocol integration but face less competition.

What's Coming

Scheduler Changes

Solana's transaction scheduler is evolving. The shift from sequential to parallel scheduling (already in progress) changes how priority fees affect ordering. MEV bots will need to adapt their fee strategies to the new scheduling model.

More Block Engine Competition

We expect 2-3 more block engines to launch in 2026, further fragmenting the submission landscape. This will increase the importance of multicast submission and per-engine optimization.

AI-Assisted Strategy Discovery

Early experiments are using machine learning to discover non-obvious MEV opportunities — patterns in order flow that human-designed heuristics miss. This is still early but could become a significant factor within 12 months.

Restaking and MEV

As restaking protocols launch on Solana, new MEV surfaces will emerge around validator selection, restaking rewards, and slash-related liquidations.

Key Takeaways for Builders

  1. Multi-engine submission is mandatory. Single-engine strategies leave money on the table.
  2. Flash loans changed the economics. Capital is no longer the primary advantage — infrastructure is.
  3. Long-tail tokens are where the alpha is. Major pairs are competed down to razor-thin margins.
  4. Latency still wins. Despite all the infrastructure, the fastest bot still captures the most value.
  5. Rust or go home. The performance gap between Rust and everything else is the difference between profitable and not.
